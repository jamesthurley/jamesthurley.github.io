title = "The Second-Best Monitor for Programming"
date = 2025-01-07

I've been writing software for about 25 years now, and messing around on computers for even longer.
In that time I've experimented with many different monitor setups.

I have fond memories of the Iiyama Vision Master Pro CRT monitors in the 90s, and my first LCD screen that I got off eBay
around the year 2000 as a student.

When I worked in an office a decade or so ago it was standard there to have one, maybe two screens.
But by gathering any spare monitors I came across I managed to accumulate four screens, attracting both
interest and raised eyebrows from colleagues, and managers, passing by my cubicle.

More recently I've had three monitors on arms: A trusty old 27" 2560x1440 Dell in portrait off to the left, a 27" 4k LG dead center,
and a random 21" monitor we had lying around sitting underneath the LG, plus the laptop screen to the right.

But juggling multiple monitors has its downsides. As I've grown older I've started to value simplicity over extravagance,
so I decided a couple of years ago to try having just one large monitor.

This was my shortlist of LG IPS monitors, priced as they were in July 2022 in the UK:

| Model         | Diagonal | Width  | Height | Price      | Resolution    |
|---------------|----------|--------|--------|------------|---------------|
| LG 40WP95C-W  | 40"      | 947mm  | 419mm  | £1,499.98  | 5k x 2k       |
| LG 32UN880-B  | 32"      | 714mm  | 420mm  | £599.98    | 4k x 2k       |
| LG 49WL95C-WE | 49"      | 1215mm | 366mm  | £1299.98   | 5k x 1440     |
| LG 43UN700    | 43"      | 967mm  | 575mm  | £679.98    | 4k x 2k       |

I've focused here on LG to keep the list short. They often supply the panels to other brands anyway.
For example there are some Dell equivalents to the above, and they use LG panels. They usually have some extra features
and a higher price tag.

I generally choose IPS monitors because I edit photos, and want good color gamut coverage.

One interesting observation from the above table is the 40" is monitor is just the 32" monitor with 25% extra width,
in both physical size and resolution, but for a whopping £900 more.

I shared my thoughts at work and my colleague Rowland kindly expanded on the table (MP = megapixels):

| Model      | MP   | m²   | £/MP | £/m² | MP/m² |
|------------|------|------|------|------|-------|
| 40WP95C-W  | 11.1 | 0.40 | 136  | 3778 | 28    |
| 32UN880-B  | 8.3  | 0.30 | 72   | 1999 | 28    |
| 49WL95C-WE | 7.4  | 0.44 | 176  | 2925 | 17    |
| 43UN700    | 8.3  | 0.56 | 82   | 1223 | 15    |

He observed that *"the 43 is great value considering its enormous size"*, and he makes a good point.
It has by far the best £/m² and the second best £/MP.

The low 15 MP/m² (pixel density) was concerning, but it was only slightly lower than my 27" 2560x1440 screen (17 MP/m²) which I was
currently using alongside a beautifully sharp 4k 27". I certainly wouldn't want to go any lower than that,
but it was close enough to risk giving it a try.

So I went for it. I've now been using the LG 43" monitor for over two years, and absolutely love it.

Compared to my past multi-monitor setups it's like having a vast canvas to spread your work out on,
rather than a restrictive series of small boxes.

I'm no longer constantly adjusting the horizontal and vertical splits in VSCode to make more room on the
pane I'm working on: There is ample room for the explorer pane and two spacious text panes horizontally,
and a terminal window at the bottom. And I *still* have horizontal space left on either side for other windows,
or even MacOS widgets (which prior to this monitor always felt rather pointless).

[![screenshot of my 43" monitor as I write this article](43-inch-monitor-screenshot.jpg)](43-inch-monitor-screenshot.jpg)

The only thing I would really change about this setup is the resolution. The resolution is *fine*, but a 6k version would be amazing.
Maybe with a slight curve, but that would probably just make it too expensive.

However, because of the resolution, there is one other monitor that in my list above that I would probably rather have: 
the `LG 40WP95C-W`. Size-wise it is the same width as my 43", but it crops off the bottom 15cm
where my terminal tends to sit. I could probably live with that. `Cmd+J` quickly shows and hides the terminal anyway. 

To make up for that slight vertical crop it has a 5k horizontal resolution rather than the 4k of the 43", and squeezes the
same 2k vertical resolution into that shorter height.
And it's curved, which I expect would be nice at these sizes.
And Dell recently released the `Dell U4025QW` version which supports 120Hz, and so probably *is* worth the extra
cost (it's £1,578.00 at time of writing) over the LG if you're spending that much anyway.

But I'm not willing to spend £1500 on a monitor, so for now I'm happy to stick with the second best monitor for programming. 
So much so that I've bought two, one for each location I work.
I only paid £483.99 for the second one I ordered (now called the `43UN700P`) in June 2024, as it was on sale.

That makes, in my opinion, the `LG 43UN700P` the *best value* monitor 
for programming at about a third of the cost of the *best* monitor, the `Dell U4025QW`.
